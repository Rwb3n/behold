# Curiosity

Decide where to direct attention next. Not how to analyze (thinking) or whether it's good (evaluation), but what's worth looking at. The highest-leverage judgment in a curiosity amplifier — choosing the right thread to pull produces nonlinear returns.

## When to use
- After completing an investigation or ingestion — what thread did I notice but not follow?
- When a session lacks direction — what's the most interesting unexplored territory?
- After reading data, logs, or results — what's surprising here?
- When multiple directions are open — which has the highest information gain?

## Guiding questions
1. What's the most surprising thing in what I just learned? Surprise = gap between expectation and reality. That gap is where new knowledge lives.
2. What thread did I notice but not follow? Every investigation produces side threads. Which one is nagging?
3. If I had to explain what I just found to someone with no context, what would they ask about? Their question is the curiosity signal — they'd ask about what's genuinely non-obvious.
4. What adjacent territory haven't I looked at? Not "what's the next step in my plan" but "what's nearby that I haven't explored?"
5. What would change my mind about the current direction? The thing that could falsify your current thesis is the highest-value thing to investigate.

## Anti-patterns
- **Don't confuse curiosity with distraction.** Curiosity follows threads that connect to existing knowledge. Distraction jumps to unrelated shiny things.
- **Don't optimize for novelty.** The most interesting thread isn't always the newest — sometimes it's an old observation that new data finally makes testable.
- **Don't skip the "so what?"** After identifying what's interesting, articulate why: what decision would change if this thread yields results?

## Output structure
- Most surprising observation from current context
- Threads noticed but not followed (ranked by potential information gain)
- Adjacent unexplored territory
- Recommended next thread to pull (with "so what?" articulated)
